Meta knowledge - What did I learn from my master research project?

Author: Alice Dauphin
Date: 05/07/20

The context

After five months of research internship in cognitive modelling I finished the writing of my master thesis a couple of weeks ago. It appeared to me that I made many mistakes and that the scientific process can be improved. The article aims at clarifying the mistakes and writing down possible solutions for future work.
Additional comment: It was the second master thesis I wrote (the first one was in developmental experimental psychology, last year).

Meta-knowledge I acquired

I was too superficial, far more that I would admit.

What do I mean by “being too superficial”?

I identified the “superficiality problem” when I had to write the Methods section of the thesis (the instructions specified that the master thesis should be organised as a paper with Introduction/Methods/Results/Discussion sections). I started to concretely think to the research problem I wanted to tackle and realised that I did not distinguished between the high level, almost philosophical questions I had from the scientific problem I could solve during my internship (Scott Aaronson in Lex Fridman’s podcast from 00:00 to 03:20 made a clear picture of the distinction).

As my research problem was not clearly defined (although I read Luc Beaudoin’s blogpost and was aware of the necessity of a problem-driven approach instead of a method/domain-driven approach) I could not see that I was not making progress. When I read papers/chapters or listened to conferences I did not truly try to compare their approaches to mine because I couldn’t: my approach did not generate any new idea, predict any new phenomenon or offer a fresh view on known effects! It was blindingly obvious but I couldn’t see it until I had to organise my thinking into a report!

Here are a couple of behaviours that should have alerted me (an have to be monitored in future work): {1} When I plotted graphs, I did not read and interpreted them in great details, namely, I did not generate testable hypotheses from them. {2} I did not generate alternative scenarios to explain my data. {3} Much more exploration (i.e. simulations and observation of the phenomenon I was studying in my everyday life) could have been done.

A possible solution: the MVP approach

The best solution I see to avoid superficiality is to implement the Minimum Viable Product (MVP) approach. In a research context it means to write, to program, and to present your work as soon as possible (in the present work I realised that I had been too superficial each time I had to do one of these actions). The main difficulty being to assign deadlines to myself.

A necessary step: become obsessed with my research problem

I’ve just realised that I selected this internship for the methods it offered me (mainly to work with a cognitive architecture which at the time was the Holy Grail for me) not for the questions I could delve into. As I was not driven by a problem I couldn’t be concerned with solving it and did not become obsessed with it. The direct result was that I failed to be goal-oriented in my readings. As I was not developing a solution I could compare with other researchers approach I was overwhelmed by them.
